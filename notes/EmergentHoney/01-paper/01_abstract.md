# Abstract

Modern cyber deception systems rely on statically configured honeypots that lose value once adversaries recognize them. We present **EmergentHoney**, a decentralized cyber deception framework in which honeypot deployment, migration, and mutation are governed by ant-colony-style pheromone dynamics rather than a central controller. The paper's primary contribution is a pheromone-driven self-organizing topology protocol that turns swarm intelligence into the operational logic of the deception system itself. We further use an LLM-based phenotype layer and a lightweight reverse-trajectory predictor as supporting modules that improve realism and timing, but do not replace the core swarm mechanism. To quantify collective behavior, we define the *Deception Emergence Index (DEI)* and analyze conditions under which coordinated honeypots outperform the sum of isolated honeypots. In a 72-hour SDN-informed emulation study on 50-, 200-, and 500-node network topologies, EmergentHoney achieves **3.24 ± 0.16 hours** average attacker dwell time, versus **1.66 ± 0.10 hours** for the best adaptive RL baseline, while reducing honeypot identification rate by **71%**. The ablation study shows that removing the pheromone mechanism causes the largest degradation (**-50.3%** ADT, DEI < 1), supporting the claim that decentralized self-organization is the central source of performance.
